1. Imagine that you have trained your dog, St. Bernard Bernie, to carry a box of 10 floppy disks (1.44MB capacity each, assume 1024 bytes = 1kb, 1000kb = 1MB) instead of a flask of brandy. The dog can travel to your side, wherever you may be, at a speed of 18 km/hour. For what range of distances does Bernie have a higher data rate than a 28 kbps telephone line?

(kbps = kilobit per second, 1 byte = 8 bit)

 

Solution:

            Assume the range of distance is d km, then the data transfer date for this dog is equal to the number of bits divided by the time needed for the transfer of data.

Step 1: Calculate number of bits in the box of 10 floppy disks:

            10 * 1.44 * 1000 * 1024 * 8 = 117964800 bits

Step 2: Calculate the time needed for the transfer:

            d km / (18 km/ 3600 seconds) = 200d seconds

Step 3: Calculate the data transfer rate, which is

            117964800 bits / 200d

                        It should be greater than 28 kbps:

            117964800 bits / 200d > 28 * 1000 bits/second

 

Solve this inequality gives the answer:

            d < 21.1 Km

 

 

2. Consider the following scheme used to compress text. The most common 200 words in the text are encoded in 8-bit byte as the numbers 0 through 199. Codes 200 through 249 could be used to indicate that the next byte contained a word code. This system allows the 200 most common words to be encoded in 1 byte, and the next 12,800 in 2 bytes. The remaining six codes, 250 through 255, could be used to indicate that ASCII string follows, with the length in the next byte. Suppose that a text consists of words (and spaces) only, with the most common 200 words accounting for 75% of all words, and the next 12,800 words accounting for 10% of all words. If the average word has k characters, find the percent savings as a function of k.

 

Solution:

The first 200 words can be encoded/saved with one byte. The next 12,800 words can be encoded with two bytes.

An ASCII string of length k can be saved with (k + 2) bytes. One extra byte needs to indicate that an ASCII string follows, and the second extra byte needs to specify the length of this ASCII string.

The number of bytes needed under this encoding scheme is

 (0.75 * 1 + 0.10 * 2 + 0.15 * (2 + k) ) = 1.25 + 0.15k

The number of bytes needed without this encoding is k bytes.

The percent savings is [(k – (1.25 + 0.15k)) / k]*100 = [0.85 – 1.25/k]*100

If k is equal to 2, the saving is 12.5%

If k is equal to 10, the saving is 72.5%

If k is equal to 20, the saving is 78.75%
